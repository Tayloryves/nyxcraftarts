Callanish:

A Central Presence

Summary

Callanish is a Celtic Fusion band based in Salt Lake City, UT. Though active on social media, they wanted a dedicated space on the web where they could centralize their presence.

Being a designer (and the vocalist *ahem*), I pounced on the opportunity, excited to employ all of my burgeoning skills as a UX/UI Designer to make something unique, powerful, and engaging.

Role

I was excited to wear the big hat for this project and assume responsibility for all of its facets.

As the primary designer, it was my duty to ensure as much effective work for my clients as possible within a short, sprint-based span of three weeks. In those weeks, I checked in regularly with my team, who were working on their own individual projects in order to give and receive constructive feedback and moral support.

My responsibilities ran the gamut of a UX/UI Design sprint, from the general research that arose from first ideations, SWOT Analyses, and Client Interviews to the further use of Surveys, Card Sorting, Usability, and A/B Testing. For the UI, I used Figma to build the initial wireframes, and found it capable of doing all the things I needed from then all the way up to the medium-fidelity prototype which amounted by the end of the sprint.

One client consultation, screener survey, and audience with the Fairy Queen later...

I was able to conjure up a scope through which I could sustain the primary focus of our vision.

Challenge

Create a user friendly website that provides a beautiful, modern aesthetic and a user-friendly interface.

Audience

Fans of Live and Celtic Music, ages 30 and up.

Outcome


The finished product fulfilled the expectations of both Client and Fanbase.

We focused on an interface that reflected the crisp, light, and airy nature inherent in so much of the genre.

Process

The Survey

I found that many of my participants wanted their bands to have some kind of website, not just Social Media accounts or an Apple Music page. Nearly all of them, including the true fans of Celtic music, expressed a longing for better representation and diversity within the live music scene in Salt Lake City.

And of course! Why not? Celtic music: It's catchy, it bursts with passion and joie de vivre. It calms the soul, expunges the pain of the heart. So yes, diversity is a given, yet amongst the fans of Callanish, one of their biggest draws is our celebration of diversity and full representation within the music and the culture.

About 60% out of 22 respondents attend live music events every 3-6 months.

Nearly a 70% of concert goers look to see if the band performing has a website.

When visiting a band’s website, nearly 90% of respondents want to see their concert schedule.

About 80% are interested in getting to know the band and hearing more of their material.

Here's what some respondents had to say:

"I like when the band makes an effort to reflect their personality, even in, well, especially when it comes to little things that other artists don't think are that important."

"I can’t stand when a band’s website is cluttered."

"I get disappointed when all I see is a boring website that looks thrown-together."

On the Shoulders of Giants

Although technically "competitive analysis" is what we UX/UI designers call this stage of the work, the nature of this particular project felt more collaborative, or inspirational if anything. My intention was to look at a few other band websites to see what they did, and I used a typical SWOT protocol to do so; however, since Callanish believes more in collaboration that we do competition, I elected to drop the "T" which, in this case, stands for "Threat".

Usability

Overall, everyone tested found the layout and interactivity to communicate what they needed to quite well.

A small portion of participants requested a scroll bar on the landing page, while others scrolled instinctually.
Still, the scroll bar offered better indication of the page's capabilities and proved more accessible.

Contrast was a point of concern for some participants, so for the second round of testing, I included panels at different value levels and asked participants to determine which was easier to read.

Insights

A web page is like a business card: Your first impression.

Accessibility is Benevolence.

Empathy is Everything,
Even for a landing page.

An espresso in time saves nine.

Fae Queens make odd requests.
